Overview of the Fruit

Mangoes are tropical fruits known for their juicy, sweet, and sometimes tangy flesh. They are oval-shaped and vary in color from green to yellow, red, and orange. The pulp is typically orange or yellow and surrounds a large, flat pit.

Mangoes are native to South Asia and are now grown in many tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. They are rich in vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants, making them a popular choice for fresh consumption, smoothies, desserts, and various culinary dishes.

Vitamins

Vitamin C

  • Role: Boosts the immune system, aids in skin health, and acts as an antioxidant.
  • Amount: Mangoes provide a significant percentage of the daily recommended intake of Vitamin C.

Vitamin A

  • Role: Supports vision health, immune function, and skin health. Mangoes are rich in beta-carotene, which the body converts to Vitamin A.

Folate (Vitamin B9)

  • Role: Crucial for DNA synthesis and repair, and important during pregnancy for fetal development.

Vitamin E

  • Role: Acts as an antioxidant and supports immune function.

Planting Tips

Climate

  • Frost sensitivity: They are sensitive to frost, so in colder climates, you may need to grow them in pots that can be moved indoors during winter.

Sunlight

  • Sunlight: Mango trees need plenty of sunlight. Aim for at least 8-10 hours of direct sunlight per day.
  • Partial Shade: In extremely hot climates, some afternoon shade can help prevent sunburn.

Soil

  • Well-Draining: Use a well-draining soil mix. Mangoes prefer slightly sandy soil. If you're using regular garden soil, mix in sand or perlite to improve drainage.
  • pH: Mangoes prefer slightly acidic to neutral soil (pH 5.5-7.5).

Watering

  • Consistent Moisture: Keep the soil consistently moist, but not waterlogged. Allow the top inch of soil to dry out between waterings.
  • Reduce Water in the Winter: Water less frequently during the winter months when the plant's growth slows down.

Fertilizing

  • Balanced Fertilizer: Use a balanced fertilizer (e.g., 10-10-10) or one high in potassium and phosphorus to promote blooming and fruiting.
  • Frequency: Fertilize every 2-3 months during the growing season (spring and summer).

Support Structure

  • Pruning: Prune the tree to remove dead or weak branches and encourage new growth. Pruning also helps to maintain a manageable size and shape.

Pollination

  • Self-Pollinating: Most mango varieties are self-pollinating, but some may benefit from cross-pollination to increase fruit set.

Pest and Disease Control

  • Watch for Pests: Keep an eye out for pests like scale insects, mealybugs, and spider mites. Treat infestations promptly with appropriate insecticides or natural remedies.
  • Disease Prevention: Ensure good airflow around the plant to prevent fungal diseases. Avoid overhead watering to reduce humidity around the plant.

Harvesting

  • Timing: Mangoes are typically ready to harvest 100-150 days after flowering. The fruit will change color (depending on the variety) and the skin will become softer.
  • Ripeness: The fruit should be firm but slightly soft when squeezed. Harvest by cutting the fruit from the tree with a sharp knife.
